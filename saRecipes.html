Feijoada, is the quintessential Brazilian dish, a hearty stew of black beans and a mix of meats, which in Brazil, is not just a meal but also a cultural phenomenon. This dish is often served on Saturdays and is meant to be a leisurely meal that can last into the evening, where people can enjoy each other's company. In the 1840s, feijoada was associated with happiness, celebratory events, drinking, and the fraternization between men and women.

Asado is a tradtional Argentinian barbecue that involves slowly cooking meat over coals. The meat is usually beef, prok, chicken, chorizo, or morcilla. Asado is not just a meal, but also a social and cultural event that brings people together. The word "asado" refers to both the meal and the gathering, and has been a part of the culture since the days of the first colonies. It is a way to honor the gauchos, the nomadic horsemen and cattle herders who roamed the pampas in the 19th century, and a way to respect the land & country.

Chilean beef empanadas, or empanadas de pino, are an iconic dish that combine both Spanish and Mapuche indigenous cultures. The dish originated during the Spanish conquest of Chile in the 1600s, when Mapuche cooks began making empanadas with ingredients introduced by the Spanish. The result is a hybrid dish called pino, which is then stuffed inside a pastry. Empanadas are so ingrained in Chile's culture, that they are often served at celebrations and special events, as families & friends gather to share plates of empanadas together.
